Child's rental home elevator death on NC coast may lead to safety changes

Tags:
  • WRAL Investigates,
  • elevator,
  • vacation rental,
  • family travel

Posted May 26, 2022 6:00 p.m. EDT
Updated May 26, 2022 6:55 p.m. EDT

By Cullen Browder, WRAL anchor/reporter

Corolla, N.C. — A continuing WRAL Investigation could lead to a change in state law regarding elevators in short-term rental properties, which is a perk for high-end beach homes.

Just 30 minutes into last summer’s family vacation in the town of Corolla, 7-year-old Weston Androw was playing hide-and-seek when he was crushed between the inner and outer doors of the beach home’s elevator.

"It’s an unspeakable, unspeakable tragedy. They’re in a lot of pain," said Raleigh attorney Stacy Miller, who represents the Androw family of Ohio as they try to honor Weston’s memory.

Miller said Weston’s death was not an isolated incident.

"Prior to this event with Weston, there had been eight cases of children getting entrapped in elevators," Miller said. "Of those eight, five children died."

A bill filed this week in the North Carolina Senate entitled "Weston’s Law" could help change that. Currently, residential elevators don’t require annual inspection like commercial ones in office buildings or hotels.

The proposed law would target homes used for short-term rentals to require a space guard that fills the gap where children have gotten trapped. It would also mandate yearly inspections paid by property owners. Last year, North Carolina Labor Commission Josh Dobson told WRAL Investigates residential elevator rules needed to change. Now, that may happen.

Elevators popular at beach rental homes, but some argue they're an accident waiting to happen

Miller hopes the Androw family’s involvement in the bill will help it pass.

"Legislation is a piece of paper. This puts a face on this legislation," Miller said. "This demonstrates the tragedy that’s happened and the tragedy that will happen if we don’t act."

Miller said Weston’s family won’t stop here or elsewhere until lives are saved.

"They intend to show up, if they have to, to committee hearings in Virginia, South Carolina [and] Florida to tell their story, and it’s a compelling story," Miller said. "They do not want this to happen again."
